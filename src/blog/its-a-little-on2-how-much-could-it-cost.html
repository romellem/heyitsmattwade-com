When I was a kid, I used to love the Price is Right. I loved the games, the prizes, and the excitement of watching people win. But there was one game that always confused me: It's in the Bag.

The premise of the game is simple: contestants are shown a series of grocery items and their prices, and they have to guess which items are in a bag that has a total price. The catch is that the prices are not always what they seem, and contestants can end up with a bag full of items that cost more than they expected.

I remember watching the show as a kid and thinking, "How hard can it be to guess the prices of these items? It's not like they're hidden or anything." But as I grew older and started to learn more about algorithms and data structures, I realized that the problem was much more complex than it seemed.

The problem with guessing prices is that it's not just about knowing the prices of individual items. It's about understanding how those prices interact with each other. For example, if you know that a loaf of bread costs $2, and a gallon of milk costs $3, you might think that a bag containing both items would cost $5. But what if the store has a sale on bread, and it's actually only $1? Suddenly, your guess is off by $1.

This is where algorithms come in. Algorithms are sets of instructions for solving problems, and they can help us make better guesses about prices. For example, we could use an algorithm to calculate the average price of an item based on its historical prices. Or we could use an algorithm to predict how prices will change over time based on factors like supply and demand.
